Lesson 12.1: Square roots & simplifying radicals

Here's a picture to start from. A square has area 9. What's the length of one side? You're looking for a number that, multiplied by itself, gives 9 вҖ” and that's 3, because 3 Г— 3 = 9. Squaring a number asks "what's the area of a square with this side?" The square root asks the reverse: "the area is 9, so what's the side?" That reverse question is the whole idea of this lesson, and it's the exact tool you'll need later to undo an xВІ. Without it, the answer to xВІ = 7 would have no name to write down.

Squaring and taking a square root undo each other, the same way the 2 power and the root sit on opposite sides of the move. So вҲҡ9 = 3 because 3ВІ = 9, and вҲҡ25 = 5 because 5ВІ = 25. When the number under the root is a perfect square вҖ” some whole number squared вҖ” the root comes out as a tidy whole number.

But not every number is that friendly. вҲҡ9 is a clean 3, yet вҲҡ7 is irrational: its decimal runs on forever without ever repeating, so no fraction lands exactly on it. That doesn't make вҲҡ7 unfinished or wrong. вҲҡ7 is the exact answer, the same way 1/3 is exact even though its decimal never stops. (This is the number system from Unit 1.2 coming back.) Reach for a rounded decimal like 2.6457 only when a problem actually asks for one; otherwise the radical is the finished, honest answer.

When a radical isn't a perfect square, you can often make it simpler without rounding it at all. The move is to split the number under the root into a perfect-square factor times whatever is left, because the perfect square can step outside the root:

$$\sqrt{12}=\sqrt{4\cdot 3}=\sqrt4\,\sqrt3=2\sqrt3$$

Read that left to right: 12 is 4 times 3, the вҲҡ4 becomes a plain 2 out front, and the вҲҡ3 stays behind because 3 has no perfect-square factor to give up. One tip that saves steps: hunt for the largest perfect-square factor. If you pull out 4 from вҲҡ72 you get 2вҲҡ18 and you're not done; pull out 36 and you land on 6вҲҡ2 in one step. Either route reaches the same place; pulling the largest just gets there fastest.

Read each worked example slowly, one line at a time, and ask why each line follows from the one above before you go on. That's what makes a worked example teach.

New terms:

  • #12.1.d2 Radical / radicand: the вҲҡ( ) is the radical; the number under it is the radicand.
  • #12.1.d3 Perfect square: a number that's some integer squared вҖ” 1,4,9,16,25,36,49,...
  • #12.1.d4 Simplified radical: no perfect-square factor left under the root. вҲҡ12=вҲҡ(4В·3)=2вҲҡ3 is simplified; вҲҡ12 is not.
  • #12.1.d5 Like radicals: radicals with the same radicand (2вҲҡ3 and 4вҲҡ3) вҖ” they add like the "boxes" of Unit 2's like terms.

One more, one power up, so you've seen it: a cube root undoes cubing. вҲӣ27 = 3 because 3Ві = 27 вҖ” same idea, just the 3 power instead of the 2.

Here's a clean case to get the method moving before the practice mixes things up. Simplify вҲҡ20. It's 4 times 5, and 4 is a perfect square, so вҲҡ20 = вҲҡ(4В·5) = 2вҲҡ5. One factor out, one left behind вҖ” that's the whole move.

It isn't a mistake; it's the exact number, in the same family as 1/3, whose decimal also never stops. The cure is to leave the radical alone unless a decimal is asked for. The second: stopping before the radical is fully simplified, like writing вҲҡ72 = 2вҲҡ18 and walking away. There's still a perfect square (9) hiding under that вҲҡ18. A quick self-check after any simplify: ask "is there still a perfect-square factor inside?" If yes, keep pulling вҖ” вҲҡ72 = 2вҲҡ18 = 2В·3вҲҡ2 = 6вҲҡ2, until none is left. Check for understanding (transfer):

You can now read вҲҡ( ) as the side of a square with a given area, simplify a radical by pulling out its largest perfect-square factor, add radicals that share a radicand, and treat an irrational root as the exact answer it is.

Lesson 12.2: The simplest quadratics (xВІ=9 вҶ’ two solutions)

Here's the surprise that runs through the rest of the unit, in its plainest form. The equation xВІ = 9 asks: what number, squared, gives 9? Try 3 вҖ” yes, 3ВІ = 9. But now try вҲ’3: (вҲ’3)ВІ is also 9. Two different starting numbers, one ended-up answer. Squaring lost the sign: it threw away whether you began on the positive side or the negative side, so both 3 and вҲ’3 are honest answers, and you have to report both.

That one fact вҖ” squaring loses the sign вҖ” is the whole unit in a sentence. It's why a quadratic usually has two solutions, why a little Вұ shows up in every method ahead, and (in the last lesson) why the U-shaped graph crosses the axis twice. Keep it in mind and the rest will feel like one idea, not a pile of separate rules.

So to solve xВІ = 9 you take the square root of both sides, but you write the Вұ yourself to put back the sign that squaring erased:

$$x^2=9\;\Rightarrow\; x=\pm\sqrt9=\pm3.$$

That Вұ is just shorthand for "two answers at once": x = Вұ3 means x = 3 or x = вҲ’3. This whole move has a name вҖ” the Square-Root Property вҖ” and it has two branches, depending on the sign of the number you're taking the root of. When that number is zero or positive, you get the two roots (collapsing to a single x = 0 when the number is 0). When it's negative, there's no real solution at all, because no real number squared can land on a negative. That second branch isn't a special exception to patch over later; it's half of the rule, and it comes straight back in Lesson 12.5.

The negative branch is worth seeing once now so it doesn't rattle you later. Take xВІ = вҲ’4. No real number squared gives вҲ’4 вҖ” every real square is zero or positive (2ВІ = 4 and (вҲ’2)ВІ = 4; nothing real reaches вҲ’4). So the answer is "no real solution," and that is the answer. It's a finished, correct result, not a sign you did something wrong.

Two practical notes before the examples. First, isolate the xВІ before you take a root. If you see 2xВІ = 50, divide by 2 first to get xВІ = 25, then root to get x = Вұ5 вҖ” don't root the whole thing while the 2 is still attached. Second, when the number isn't a perfect square, the answer stays in radical form, exactly as in Lesson 12.1: xВІ = 7 gives x = ВұвҲҡ7, and that's exact and done.

New terms:

  • #12.2.d1 Вұ("plus or minus"): shorthand for two answers at once. x=Вұ3 means x=3 or x=-3.
  • #12.2.d2 Quadratic equation: an equation with an xВІ term (and no higher power). Standard form is axВІ+bx+c=0 with aвү 0 вҖ” these are the special case where b=0.
  • #12.2.d3 Square-Root Property (the rule of this lesson вҖ” box it): for xВІ=k,
  • if kвүҘ0, then x=ВұвҲҡk (i.e. x=вҲҡk or x=-вҲҡk вҖ” two real solutions, collapsing to the single x=0 when k=0);
  • if k<0, there is no real solution (no real number squares to a negative).

Stated once, conditional and complete: xВІ=k вҮ’ x=ВұвҲҡk when kвүҘ0; no real solution when k<0. The k<0 branch is not a side-exception to patch later вҖ” it is half of the rule, and it returns in 12.5 as the discriminant's sign.

Worked examples:

  1. #12.2.w1 xВІ=9 вҮ’ x=Вұ3, i.e. x=3 or x=-3. Check both: 3ВІ=9, (-3)ВІ=9. Both starting numbers square to 9, which is exactly why both are answers.
  2. #12.2.w2 xВІ=16 вҮ’ x=Вұ4. Same move: 16 is 4ВІ, so the two roots are 4 and вҲ’4.
  3. #12.2.w3 xВІ-7=0 вҮ’ xВІ=7 вҮ’ x=ВұвҲҡ7 (exact, irrational вҖ” not "unfinished," per 12.1). Add 7 to both sides to isolate xВІ, then root; 7 isn't a perfect square, so вҲҡ7 stays.
  4. #12.2.w4 2xВІ=50 вҮ’ xВІ=25 вҮ’ x=Вұ5. (Isolate xВІ first вҖ” divide by 2 вҖ” before rooting.) If you rooted before dividing you'd drag along an extra radical and land on Вұ5вҲҡ2, which is wrong here.
  5. #12.2.w5 xВІ-3=0 вҮ’ xВІ=3 вҮ’ x=ВұвҲҡ3. Isolate, then root; 3 isn't a perfect square, so leave the radical.
  6. #12.2.w6 The k<0 branch (no real solution): xВІ=-4. Here k=-4<0. By the Square-Root Property there is no real solution вҖ” no real number squares to -4, since any real square is вүҘ0. (Check the intuition: 2ВІ=4 and (-2)ВІ=4; nothing real lands on -4.) The answer is "no real solution," full stop. Same for xВІ+4=0 вҮ’ xВІ=-4: isolate first, see the negative, stop.

The substitution check above is how you'll know you're right when no one's there to tell you. If you put an answer back and the two sides don't match, you haven't failed. Your check just did its job and caught something before it counted. Go back to your first step and re-run the arithmetic slowly; a mismatch is almost always one sign or one small slip, not the whole method.

Here's a clean one to get the rhythm before the set turns mixed: solve xВІ = 1. The square root of 1 is 1, so x = Вұ1, meaning x = 1 or x = вҲ’1. Check: 1ВІ = 1 and (вҲ’1)ВІ = 1. Both land, both count.

One slip deserves a callout now that you've done several correctly: writing only x = 3 for xВІ = 9 and stopping. That's the most natural mistake in the whole unit, and it comes from forgetting that squaring hid a sign. The fix is built into the method. Whenever you undo a square, write the Вұ first, before you fill in the number, so the second root can't slip away. (And if isolating ever leaves you with xВІ equal to a negative, don't force a Вұ onto it; the negative branch already told you the honest answer is "no real solution.")

Check for understanding (transfer):

  1. #12.2.c1 Solve xВІ = 49. How many answers, and why isn't it just one? (x = Вұ7, so two answers вҖ” x = 7 or x = вҲ’7 вҖ” because both 7ВІ and (вҲ’7)ВІ equal 49; squaring lost the sign, so both starts are valid.)
  2. #12.2.c2 A friend says xВІ = 20 has answer x = вҲҡ20. What did they leave out, and can you simplify the radical too? (They left out the negative root: it's x = ВұвҲҡ20. And вҲҡ20 = вҲҡ(4В·5) = 2вҲҡ5, so the full answer is x = Вұ2вҲҡ5.)

You can now solve xВІ = k by isolating the xВІ, writing the Вұ to keep both signs when k is zero or positive, and recognizing "no real solution" as the correct, finished answer when k is negative.

Lesson 12.3: Solving by factoring (zero-product property)

Start with something you already trust about multiplying. If you multiply two numbers and the result is 0, one of them had to be 0 вҖ” there's simply no other way to reach zero. Two non-zero numbers never multiply to zero. That small, obvious fact is the engine of this lesson.

Turn it on an equation. Suppose (x вҲ’ 2)(x вҲ’ 3) = 0. You've got two things multiplied together giving 0, so one of them must be 0: either x вҲ’ 2 = 0 or x вҲ’ 3 = 0. Each of those is a little one-step equation you can already solve вҖ” x = 2 from the first, x = 3 from the second. So the quadratic has two solutions, 2 and 3, and you found them by reading off the factors. This is the zero-product property, and it's the everyday method for the many quadratics that factor.

The one thing to hold onto: this trick works only against zero. Zero is special вҖ” it's the single value a product can reach only by one of its parts being zero. A product equal to 8 tells you nothing useful about the factors, because lots of pairs multiply to 8. So the first move, always, is to get the equation into the form (something) = 0, with everything on one side.

Each factor-equals-zero gives a genuine solution, not just a candidate, and it's worth seeing why. The property runs both ways. Forward: if the product is 0, some factor is 0 вҖ” that's what finds your x-values. Backward: if a factor is 0, then the whole product is 0, so that x truly makes the equation true. The substitution check at the end is exactly that backward direction confirming the root is real. And the factoring step itself is the reverse-distribute, area-box move from Unit 11, run to find the edges; once you've got the factors, each one set to zero is its own tiny equation.

So the procedure has four beats: get one side to 0 in the form axВІ + bx + c = 0, factor the quadratic (Unit 11), set each factor equal to 0, and solve each little equation.

New terms:

  • #12.3.d1 Zero-product property: if a product equals 0, then at least one factor is 0. If AВ·B=0, then A=0 or B=0. (No other number forces a product to zero вҖ” this is special to 0.)
  • #12.3.d2 Root / solution: a value of x that makes the equation true вҖ” the same "roots" that are x-intercepts in 12.6.

Worked examples:

  1. #12.3.w1 (x-2)(x-3)=0. Already factored and equal to 0. Set each: x-2=0 вҮ’ x=2; x-3=0 вҮ’ x=3. x=2 or x=3. Check: (2-2)(2-3)=0В·(-1)=0. The first factor went to zero, which is enough to make the whole product zero.
  2. #12.3.w2 xВІ-5x+6=0. Factor first (what multiplies to +6, adds to -5? -2,-3): (x-2)(x-3)=0 вҮ’ x=2 or 3. Same two roots as #1, reached by factoring before reading them off.
  3. #12.3.w3 xВІ+x-6=0. (Multiply to -6, add to +1: +3,-2.) (x+3)(x-2)=0 вҮ’ x=-3 or 2. Notice x+3=0 gives x=-3, not +3 вҖ” the factor and its root carry opposite signs.
  4. #12.3.w4 xВІ-9=0. Difference of squares (Unit 11.3): (x-3)(x+3)=0 вҮ’ x=Вұ3. (Same answer 12.2 gives вҖ” two routes, one truth.)
  5. #12.3.w5 Must equal 0 first: xВІ-5x=-6 is not ready. Move the -6 over: xВІ-5x+6=0, then factor as in #2 вҶ’ x=2,3. Setting factors of xВІ-5x=-6 to numbers is meaningless, because the zero-product property only works against 0.

Here's a clean case to settle the method before the set mixes things up: xВІ вҲ’ x = 0. Both terms share an x, so factor it out: x(x вҲ’ 1) = 0. Then x = 0 or x вҲ’ 1 = 0, giving x = 0 or x = 1. Check: 0ВІ вҲ’ 0 = 0, and 1ВІ вҲ’ 1 = 0. Two roots, and one of them is zero itself, which is perfectly allowed.

Now that several are solved, two slips are worth a word. The first is setting factors equal to the wrong number, factoring xВІ вҲ’ 5x + 6 = 8 and writing x вҲ’ 2 = 8. The property needs a zero on the other side; an 8 gives you nothing, so move everything over first to make that side 0. The second is stopping after one factor, reporting x = 2 and forgetting x = 3. Each factor is its own equation and (usually) its own root, so finish both вҖ” two factors, two answers. (If your factoring ever feels shaky, you can always check it by expanding the factors back out, or by substituting a root into the original; both catch a sign error at the factoring step.)

Check for understanding (transfer):

  1. #12.3.c1 Solve (x + 5)(x вҲ’ 1) = 0 without expanding. What lets you split it into two easy equations? (The zero-product property: the product is 0, so a factor is 0 вҖ” x + 5 = 0 or x вҲ’ 1 = 0, giving x = вҲ’5 or x = 1.)
  2. #12.3.c2 A solution factors xВІ вҲ’ 2x вҲ’ 8 = 0 into (x вҲ’ 4)(x + 2) and stops at x = 4. What's missing? (The second factor: x + 2 = 0 gives x = вҲ’2, so the full answer is x = 4 or x = вҲ’2. Each factor yields a root.)

Lesson 12.4: Completing the square

Start with a fact you can check by hand: (x + 3)ВІ = xВІ + 6x + 9. Look at that constant, the 9. It's exactly (6/2)ВІ вҖ” half of the middle coefficient, squared. That's not a coincidence, and it's the lever for this whole lesson. If you're handed xВІ + 6x and want to make it into a perfect square, you now know precisely what to add: 9. Adding the right number to turn xВІ + bx into something-squared is called completing the square.

There's a literal picture behind the name. Think of xВІ + 6x as an x-by-x square sitting next to two strips, each 3 by x (the 6x split into two 3x pieces laid along two sides). Those pieces almost form a bigger square, but there's a corner missing вҖ” a 3-by-3 block, area 9. Drop that block in and the figure becomes a complete square, (x + 3) on each side. The algebra is doing the same thing the picture is: filling in the missing corner.

Why bother? Two reasons. It solves quadratics that won't factor with nice integers вҖ” and it's where the quadratic formula in the next lesson actually comes from. Seeing it once makes that formula feel earned instead of dropped on you.

The move itself, on an equation, is short. Get the xВІ and x terms alone on one side, add (b/2)ВІ to both sides (so the scale stays balanced вҖ” Unit 2's balance scale again), factor the left side as a square, and finish with the ВұвҲҡ( ) of Lesson 12.2. Watch the worked examples for how the balance is kept on every line.

New terms:

  • #12.4.d1 Perfect-square trinomial: a trinomial that factors as something squared вҖ” xВІ+6x+9=(x+3)ВІ (Unit 11.3).
  • #12.4.d2 Completing the square: adding the exact number, (b/2)ВІ, that turns xВІ+bx into a perfect square.

Worked examples:

  1. #12.4.w1 xВІ+6x+5=0. First move the constant aside and add (6/2)ВІ=9 to both sides to build a perfect square on the left: $$x^2+6x=-5 \;\xrightarrow{+\,(6/2)^2=9}\; x^2+6x+9=-5+9 \;\Rightarrow\; (x+3)^2=4$$ $$x+3=\pm2 \;\Rightarrow\; x=-3\pm2 \;\Rightarrow\; x=-1 \text{ or } x=-5.$$ Adding 9 to both sides keeps the equation balanced, the left collapses to (x+3)ВІ, and the Вұ at the root step recovers both solutions. Check: (-1)ВІ+6(-1)+5=0, (-5)ВІ+6(-5)+5=0.

  2. #12.4.w2 xВІ+4x-5=0. Move: xВІ+4x=5. Add (4/2)ВІ=4 to both sides: (x+2)ВІ=9 вҮ’ x+2=Вұ3 вҮ’ x=1 or -5. Half of 4 is 2, squared is 4 вҖ” that's the number that completes the square.

  3. #12.4.w3 xВІ-2x-3=0. Move: xВІ-2x=3. Add (-2/2)ВІ=1 to both sides: (x-1)ВІ=4 вҮ’ x-1=Вұ2 вҮ’ x=3 or -1. Note half of -2 is -1, so the square is (x-1)ВІ, minus sign and all.

  4. #12.4.w4 Where it earns its keep (factoring fails): xВІ-4x+1=0. Move: xВІ-4x=-1. Add (-4/2)ВІ=4 to both sides: (x-2)ВІ=3 вҮ’ x-2=ВұвҲҡ3 вҮ’ x=2ВұвҲҡ3. No integer factoring could have found this вҖ” but completing the square does, and the answer stays exact in radical form.

A clean case to lock the move in before the practice: complete the square on xВІ + 6x (just the expression, no equation to solve). Half of 6 is 3, and 3ВІ = 9, so you add 9, giving xВІ + 6x + 9 = (x + 3)ВІ. That's the heart of every example above, on its own.

You've worked the move; here are the spots people stumble. The most common is adding (b/2)ВІ to only one side, which quietly changes the equation and breaks the answer; whatever you add on the left you must add on the right, to keep the balance. The next is forgetting to halve before squaring, adding bВІ instead of (b/2)ВІ; the tell is that the left side won't fold into a clean square, so the rule is half of b first, then square. And the unit's usual one returns at the root step: write the Вұ so x + 3 = Вұ2 instead of just x + 3 = 2, or you'll lose the second root. (When b is negative, halving carries the sign: half of вҲ’2 is вҲ’1, so the square is (x вҲ’ 1)ВІ, not (x + 1)ВІ.)

Lesson 12.5: The quadratic formula & the discriminant

Factoring is quick when it works, and completing the square always works but is fiddly to redo every time. The quadratic formula is the way out: it's completing the square done once, in general, so you never have to grind through it again. You just read the three numbers a, b, and c off the equation and drop them in. It solves every quadratic вҖ” factorable or not, with rational answers or irrational ones вҖ” which is why it's the universal tool.

For any quadratic written as axВІ + bx + c = 0,

$$x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}.$$

That Вұ is the same one from every lesson in this unit вҖ” it's recovering the sign that squaring threw away. To use the formula well, read a, b, and c carefully, with their signs: for 2xВІ + 3x вҲ’ 2 = 0, a = 2, b = 3, and c = вҲ’2, where the minus genuinely belongs to the c. (If you've ever wondered where the 2a on the bottom comes from: the general derivation first divides everything by a so the xВІ coefficient becomes 1, then completes the square (Lesson 12.4), and that division is exactly what leaves the 2a in the denominator. You don't have to redo it; the formula already did.)

There's a bonus tucked inside the formula. The part under the root, bВІ вҲ’ 4ac, is the discriminant, and its sign tells you how many real solutions you'll get before you finish the arithmetic. If it's positive, the root of a positive number gives two different values, so two real solutions. If it's zero, the Вұ adds and subtracts nothing, so the two solutions collapse into one (a repeated root). If it's negative, there's no real square root to take, so no real solution. That negative case is the same "no real solution" branch you met in Lesson 12.2, now showing up as the sign of the discriminant.

One refinement when the discriminant is positive, worth knowing because it explains a pattern you've already felt. If bВІ вҲ’ 4ac is itself a perfect square, the root comes out whole and the two answers are rational вҖ” which means the quadratic would have factored over the integers (the Lesson 12.3 case). If it's positive but not a perfect square, the answers keep a radical and are irrational. That's exactly why some quadratics factor cleanly and others only yield to the formula. (For instance: xВІ вҲ’ 3x вҲ’ 10 = 0 has discriminant 49 = 7ВІ, so rational roots 5 and вҲ’2; xВІ вҲ’ 2x вҲ’ 5 = 0 has discriminant 24, so irrational roots 1 Вұ вҲҡ6.)

The working order is steady: substitute a, b, c; simplify the discriminant first; then take its root and resolve the Вұ. Then check a root by putting it back in.

  • #12.5.d1 Quadratic formula: for axВІ+bx+c=0 (with aвү 0), $$x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}.$$ The Вұ is the same two-solutions Вұ as always вҖ” recovering the sign squaring destroyed.

  • #12.5.d2 Discriminant: the inside-the-root part, bВІ-4ac. For a real quadratic (aвү 0, real coefficients) its sign counts the real solutions before you finish:

  • bВІ-4ac>0: two real solutions (root of a positive вҶ’ Вұ two values).
  • bВІ-4ac=0: one real solution вҖ” a repeated (double) root (Вұ0 collapses to one; the parabola's vertex sits on the axis).

Lesson 12.6: Graphing parabolas

Here's the picture the whole unit has been pointing at. A quadratic, graphed, is a smooth U вҖ” a parabola. The solutions you've been finding are where that U crosses the x-axis, and a U usually crosses twice: once on the way down, once on the way back up. There are your two solutions, drawn. When a quadratic has just one solution, the U barely touches the axis at its lowest point (the discriminant was 0). When it has none, the whole U floats clear of the axis, above it or below it (the discriminant was negative). The graph re-explains everything from Lesson 12.5 in one image.

That's the deep reason a quadratic usually has two solutions, and it ties the unit together: radicals, roots, the discriminant, and now the curve are all the same story. A quadratic is also just a function from Units 4 and 5 вҖ” y = xВІ вҲ’ 4 and f(x) = xВІ вҲ’ 4 are the same object, and the "roots" are simply the inputs where the output is 0, which is where the graph meets the x-axis.

To draw one well, work from numbers rather than guessing the shape. Pick a handful of x-values, compute the matching y for each, plot the points, and join them into a smooth U. The example below shows the line through those points #12.6.f1, with the roots and vertex marked, alongside the table the curve was built from.

Three features tell you most of what you need. The direction comes from the sign of a: a > 0 opens up (a valley), a < 0 opens down (a hill). The roots are where y = 0; solve the quadratic by any method from 12.3 to 12.5. The vertex is the turning point, the bottom of a valley or top of a hill; its x-coordinate is x = вҲ’b/(2a), and you plug that back in for its y. One handy check: when there are two real roots, the vertex sits exactly halfway between them, so the average of the roots gives the vertex's x. That follows from the U being a mirror image across the vertical line through its vertex вҖ” the axis of symmetry. The вҲ’b/(2a) formula stays the general tool, though; it works even when the roots are irrational or absent.

Sometimes both roots are real and only one fits the question; that's the "reject the impossible root" move, and it's where "two solutions" and "no real solution" stop being mechanics and start meaning something. Solve each by whichever method is cleanest (factoring is enough for all three below), then read your answer back into the story.

Worked example (model it, then reject a root): A ball is thrown straight up; its height in feet after t seconds is h=-16tВІ+32t. When does it hit the ground (h=0)?
