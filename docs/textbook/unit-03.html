Unit 3: Proportional Reasoning (the bridge to linearity)

Ratios, rates, unit rate, proportions, percents

Prerequisites: Unit 2 (solving one- and two-step equations, isolating a variable), and comfort with fractions — reducing, multiplying, and reading them as values on the number line. By the end, the student can:

  • Write and reduce a ratio, and find a unit rate by dividing.
  • Recognize that a unit rate is a constant rate of change — the same object that becomes slope in Unit 5.
  • Set up a proportion, explain why cross-multiplication works, and solve it with Unit 2 techniques.
  • Tell a direct proportion from an inverse one, and not auto-apply cross-multiplication to the inverse case.
  • Convert fluently among fractions, decimals, and percents, and solve all three percent types plus percent change.

Teaching this unit (orientation for the tutor)

This unit is the hinge of the whole course. Arithmetic asks "how much?"; algebra asks "how do two quantities move together?" A unit rate — 60 miles every 1 hour, \$1.50 every 1 apple — is the first time the student meets a constant rate of change, and that is exactly what slope is in Unit 5.

A note on placement: this content (ratios, proportions, percents) sits below the Algebra-1 line — it is Grade 6–7 proportional-reasoning material, not new Algebra 1. It is here deliberately, as a bridge and a review: the on-ramp to linearity, where a unit rate is a constant rate of change is slope. Treat it as consolidating and reframing what the student likely half-knows, then pointing it forward — not as introducing brand-new Algebra-1 machinery. Say this out loud and keep saying it: "the unit rate you just found is the seed of slope." Lean on metaphors.md Slope B (speed as a rate) as the through-line — a distance-vs-time graph's steepness is the miles-per-hour. By the time the student reaches 5.3, slope should feel like an old friend wearing a new name.

The arc: 3.1 builds rate/unit-rate from ratios (concrete → divide-to-get-per-1). 3.2 stacks two ratios into a proportion and earns cross-multiplication honestly (it is just "multiply both sides by both denominators," not a magic X) — then solves with Unit 2 moves. 3.3 reframes everything as "per hundred" and drills fraction ↔ decimal ↔ percent fluency, the conversion that pays off forever.

Biggest traps, by lesson:

  • 3.1 / fractions: the bigger-denominator-is-bigger reflex and the ratio reversal (writing the parts in the wrong order). See misconceptions.md §4 (fractions) and §2 (variables / the "6s = p" reversal).
  • 3.2: treating cross-multiplication as a memorized trick with no why, and — the deeper one — assuming everything proportional-looking is direct. Problem 7 is a deliberate inverse case to break that reflex.
  • 3.3: percent-of vs. what-percent confusion, and forgetting that percent change is measured against the original (the denominator is always the old value).

Pacing: most adults move briskly through 3.1; spend the real time on the why in 3.2 and on conversion fluency in 3.3. Reinforce fraction↔decimal↔percent fluency in every lesson, not just the last. Interleave a Unit 2 solve whenever a proportion bottoms out into an equation — that is the payoff of having done Unit 2 first.

Lesson 3.1: Ratios and rates

Goal: Write and reduce ratios, and find a unit rate by dividing so the second quantity becomes 1. Why it matters: A unit rate ("per one") is a constant rate of change — the literal seed of slope (Unit 5). Rates are also how the real world talks: mph, price per item, words per minute. New terms:

  • #3.1.d1 Ratio — a comparison of two quantities, written 3:2 or 3/2 or "3 to 2." The quantities can be the same kind of thing (dogs to cats).
  • #3.1.d2 Part-to-part vs part-to-whole — "8 dogs to 12 cats" (8:12) compares one part to another part. But "8 dogs out of 20 animals" (8/20) compares a part to the whole. Same animals, different comparison. This matters: percents in 3.3 are all part-to-whole (part/whole = percent/100), so get comfortable sliding from 8:12 to 8 out of 20 now.
  • #3.1.d3 Rate — a ratio comparing two unlike units (120 miles per 2 hours).
  • #3.1.d4 Unit rate — a rate whose second quantity is 1 (60 miles per 1 hour = 60 mph). You get it by dividing.

Teaching arc (concrete → pictorial → symbolic):

  • Concrete: "You drove 120 miles in 2 hours. How far in one hour, on average?" Most people split it without thinking — that splitting is finding the unit rate. Tie to metaphors.md Slope B: on a distance-vs-time picture, this number is the speed.
  • Pictorial: a simple two-column table — 2 hr → 120 mi, so 1 hr → 60 mi. "Per one" is the bottom row.
  • Symbolic: a rate is a fraction; divide top by bottom to make the denominator 1. 120 mi / 2 hr = 60 mi / 1 hr = 60 mph. Reduce a ratio exactly like a fraction: divide both parts by their common factor. Foreshadow explicitly: "Hold onto '60 miles per 1 hour' — in Unit 5 that same number is the slope of the line."

Worked examples:

  1. #3.1.w1 Unit rate (distance). 150 miles in 3 hours. $$\frac{150 \text{ mi}}{3 \text{ hr}} = \frac{150 \div 3}{3 \div 3}\,\frac{\text{mi}}{\text{hr}} = 50 \text{ mph}.$$ Divide to land on "per 1 hour."

  2. #3.1.w2 Unit price. \$6 for 4 apples. Keep the units attached — a rate compares two unlike units, so they stay in the numerator and denominator (just like WE1's mi/hr): $$\frac{\$6}{4 \text{ apples}} = \frac{\$6 \div 4}{4 \text{ apples} \div 4} = \frac{\$1.50}{1 \text{ apple}} = \$1.50 \text{ per apple}.$$ "Per apple" means the bottom must be 1 — so divide by 4. (Don't cancel the apples away: \$6 over 4 apples is a rate with units, not a bare number.)

  3. #3.1.w3 Reduce a ratio. 12:18. Both share a factor of 6: $$12:18 = \frac{12 \div 6}{18 \div 6} = 2:3.$$ The value didn't change — 12:18 and 2:3 name the same comparison, just with smaller numbers (misconceptions.md §4: reducing renames, it doesn't shrink).

Watch for:

  • Ratio reversal — writing "8 dogs to 12 cats" as 12:8. The first thing named goes first. (misconceptions.md §2, the "6s = p" reversal family.)
  • Bigger-denominator-is-bigger — surfaces when comparing rates; "\$1.50/apple vs \$2/apple, which is the better deal?" Tie value to size, not to digit count (misconceptions.md §4).
  • Forgetting to make the denominator 1 — reporting "150 mi per 3 hr" as if it were already a unit rate. The unit in unit rate means per one.

Visuals to offer: None needed — a two-row "per 1" table in chat is enough. (If a student wants to see the rate as steepness, sketch the tiny rise/run picture from visuals.md and connect it to Slope B; otherwise save graphs for Unit 5.)

Check for understanding (transfer):

  • #3.1.c1 "A printer does 90 pages in 3 minutes. What's the unit rate, and what are its units?"
  • #3.1.c2 "Two stores: 3 cans for \$4, or 5 cans for \$6. Which is cheaper per can, and how did you decide?"
  • #3.1.c3 "Why does 'unit rate' always have a 1 in it — what is the 1 counting?"
  • #3.1.c4 "There are 8 dogs and 12 cats. As a part-to-whole comparison, what fraction of those 20 animals are dogs?" (8/20 = 2/5 — turns the part-to-part ratio into the part/whole form percents will use.)

Practice problems:

Reduce ratios to lowest terms:

  1. #3.1.1 Write the ratio of 8 dogs to 12 cats in lowest terms.
  2. #3.1.2 Reduce 15:25.

Find the unit rate / unit price:

  1. #3.1.3 A car travels 180 miles in 4 hours. Find the unit rate (mph).
  2. #3.1.4 \$20 buys 5 notebooks. What does each notebook cost?
  3. #3.1.5 A typist writes 240 words in 6 minutes. Find the rate in words per minute (wpm).

Rate as a fraction (think before reducing):

  1. #3.1.6 A recipe uses 6 eggs for 24 pancakes. How many eggs per pancake? (Give a fraction.)

Answer key:

  1. 8:12 = 2:3.
  2. 180 ÷ 4 = 45 mph.
  3. \$20 ÷ 5 = \$4 each.
  4. 15:25 = 3:5.
  5. 240 ÷ 6 = 40 wpm.
  6. 6 eggs / 24 pancakes = 1 egg / 4 pancakes = 1/4 egg per pancake — keep the units on the simplified rate (it's a rate, two unlike units, not a bare 1/4). (Fewer than one egg per pancake — a fine, real fraction; misconceptions.md §4 if "less than 1" feels strange.)

Lesson 3.2: Proportions & cross-multiplication

Goal: Set up a proportion, justify cross-multiplication, solve it with Unit 2 techniques, and distinguish direct from inverse proportion. Why it matters: Proportions are the workhorse of scaling — recipes, maps, prices, unit conversions. And cross-multiplication, understood (not memorized), is a clean reuse of Unit 2's "do the same to both sides." New terms:

  • #3.2.d1 Proportion — a statement that two ratios are equal: a/b = c/d (where b and d are not 0, so the fractions are defined).
  • #3.2.d2 Cross-multiplication — for a/b = c/d with b, d ≠ 0, the rule a·d = b·c.
  • #3.2.d3 Direct proportion — as one quantity grows, the other grows in the same ratio (more apples → more cost). The ratios stay equal.
  • #3.2.d4 Inverse proportion — as one quantity grows, the other shrinks so that their product stays constant (more workers → fewer days; workers × days = total work). The constant product workers × days = k mirrors the constant ratio of the direct case; symbolically xy = k (equivalently y = k/x). (Its graph is a curve, not a line — that's out of scope for now; here we only use the constant-product idea to solve.)

Teaching arc (concrete → pictorial → symbolic):

  • Concrete: "4 apples cost \$3. At the same rate, what do 10 apples cost?" The phrase same rate is the whole idea — the price-per-apple ratio doesn't change, so two ratios are equal.
  • Pictorial: stack the two ratios as equal fractions: (4 apples)/(\$3) = (10 apples)/(\$x). (Keep units in the same position top and bottom — apples over dollars on both sides.)
  • Symbolic — earn the cross-multiply, don't conjure it. Start from a/b = c/d and use Unit 2's "do the same to both sides": $$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d} \;\xrightarrow{\times b}\; a = \frac{bc}{d} \;\xrightarrow{\times d}\; a\cdot d = b\cdot c.$$ "All we did was clear each denominator by multiplying both sides — first by b, then by d. The 'X' is the result, not a magic move." (This works because b and d are nonzero, so multiplying by them is reversible — multiplying an equation by something that could be 0 is not safe.) Then the leftover equation is a Unit 2 one-step solve. Flag clearly: cross-multiplication is for direct proportions (two equal ratios). For an inverse relationship you set the products equal instead — see practice problem 7.

Worked examples:

  1. #3.2.w1 Solve a basic proportion. x/4 = 6/8. Cross-multiply: 8x = 24, so x = 3. (Check: 3/4 = 6/8.)

  2. #3.2.w2 Variable in a denominator. 3/x = 9/15. Here x ≠ 0, since 3/x must be defined. Cross-multiply: 9x = 45, so x = 5. (Check: 3/5 = 9/15.)

  3. #3.2.w3 Word problem (direct). 4 apples cost \$3; what do 10 apples cost? Write it with the units inline so the "same unit on top on both sides" rule is visible — apples over dollars on both sides: $$\frac{4 \text{ apples}}{\$3} = \frac{10 \text{ apples}}{\$x} \;\Rightarrow\; 4x = 30 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \$7.50.$$ (Check: 4/3 = 10/7.5.)

  4. #3.2.w4 Inverse — the trap. 5 workers build a wall in 12 days. How long for 3 workers (same total work)? The total work is fixed: workers × days = 5 × 12 = 60 worker-days. So 3 × t = 60, giving t = 20 days. Fewer workers → more days — a direct-style proportion 5/3 = 12/t would be wrong here.

Watch for:

  • Cross-multiply as a magic X — the tell is a student who can do it but can't say why, and who then misapplies it (e.g., to a sum or to an inverse case). Repair by re-deriving it as "clear both denominators" (misconceptions.md §1: the relational meaning of =; misconceptions.md §7: trick-chasing).
  • Inverted/mismatched units — putting apples over dollars on one side and dollars over apples on the other. Keep the same unit on top on both sides.
  • Assuming everything is direct — the deep one. Before cross-multiplying, ask: "when this quantity goes up, does the other go up (direct) or down (inverse)?" Problem 7 trains this.

Visuals to offer: None needed; the stacked-fraction setup in chat carries it. (Save the graph of a proportional relationship — a line through the origin whose slope is the unit rate — as a forward pointer to Unit 5.)

Check for understanding (transfer):

  • #3.2.c1 "Re-derive cross-multiplication for a/b = c/d in your own steps — what are you actually doing to both sides?"
  • #3.2.c2 "A car going 60 mph covers a fixed 120-mile trip; at 40 mph it takes longer. Is trip-time-vs-speed direct or inverse? How would you set it up?"
  • #3.2.c3 "In 2/5 = x/20, without solving, will x be bigger or smaller than 2, and why?"

Practice problems:

Solve the proportion (direct):

  1. #3.2.1 x/3 = 10/15
  2. #3.2.2 2/5 = x/20
  3. #3.2.3 6/x = 3/4
  4. #3.2.4 x/8 = 9/12
  5. #3.2.5 7/2 = 21/x

Word problems (set up, then solve):

  1. #3.2.6 On a map, 1 inch represents 50 miles. How many miles is 3.5 inches?
  2. #3.2.7 5 workers build a wall in 12 days. Is this direct or inverse? Set it up and solve for how long 3 workers take at the same total work.
  3. #3.2.8 9 lb of apples cost \$18. What do 4 lb cost?

Answer key:

  1. 15x = 30 ⇒ x = 2.
  2. 40 = 5x ⇒ x = 8.
  3. 24 = 3x ⇒ x = 8.
  4. 12x = 72 ⇒ x = 6.
  5. 7x = 42 ⇒ x = 6.
  6. 1/50 = 3.5/x ⇒ x = 50 × 3.5 = 175 miles.
  7. Inverse. Total work = 5 × 12 = 60 worker-days, so 3 × t = 60, giving t = 20 days. (Not 5/3 = 12/t — fewer workers means more time.)
  8. Unit price \$18 ÷ 9 = \$2/lb, so 4 × \$2 = \$8. (Or 9/18 = 4/x ⇒ x = 8.)

Lesson 3.3: Percents

Goal: Convert fluently among fractions, decimals, and percents, and solve all three percent types plus percent change. Why it matters: Percents are proportions with a fixed denominator of 100 — the everyday face of proportional reasoning (tips, discounts, interest, statistics). Two-step percent problems (problems 9–10) are a first taste of the real-world multi-step modeling that arrives in Unit 6. New terms:

  • #3.3.d1 Percent — "per hundred"; a ratio out of 100. 25% = 25/100 = 0.25 = 1/4.
  • #3.3.d2 Percent change — how much a quantity grew or shrank relative to its original value: ((new − old)/old) × 100%. It's one signed formula: a positive result is an increase, a negative one a decrease. We report the size with the word that matches the sign — e.g. −25% is "a 25% decrease."

Teaching arc (concrete → pictorial → symbolic):

  • Concrete: "Out of every 100, how many?" A 100-square grid: 25% is 25 shaded squares. Anchor the trio 1/2 = 0.5 = 50% and 1/4 = 0.25 = 25% so conversion is reflexive.
  • Pictorial: the proportion skeleton — part/whole = percent/100. A percent is just a part-to-whole ratio (the 3.1 distinction) rescaled so the whole is 100. Every percent question fills in two of the three slots (part, whole, percent) and solves for the third with Unit 2 / 3.2 tools.
  • Symbolic: equivalently, part = percent × whole (with percent as a decimal). The three types are just which slot is unknown:
  • Find the part: 20% of 80: 0.20 × 80 = 16.
  • Find the percent: 15 is what % of 60? 15/60 = 0.25 = 25%.
  • Find the whole: 12 is 30% of what? 0.30 × w = 12 ⇒ w = 40. Then percent change uses the old value as the whole: a rise \$40 → \$50 is (50-40)/40 = 25% increase; \$80 → \$60 is (80-60)/80 = 25% decrease. Keep converting between fraction/decimal/percent the entire lesson.

Worked examples:

  1. #3.3.w1 Find the part. 20% of 80: 0.20 × 80 = 16.
  2. #3.3.w2 Find the percent. 15 is what % of 60? 15/60 = 1/4 = 0.25 = 25%.
  3. #3.3.w3 Find the whole. 12 is 30% of what? 0.30 · w = 12 ⇒ w = 12/0.30 = 40.
  4. #3.3.w4 Percent increase. \$40 → \$50: (50-40)/40 = 10/40 = 25% increase.
  5. #3.3.w5 Percent decrease. \$80 → \$60: (80-60)/80 = 20/80 = 25% decrease.

Watch for:

  • "of" auto-means-multiply, applied blindly — fine for "find the part," but a keyword reflex misfires on "what percent of" or "is 30% of what." Read the sentence, identify which slot is missing (misconceptions.md §7, keyword shortcuts).
  • Wrong base for percent change — dividing by the new value instead of the old. The denominator is always where you started. (\$40 → \$50 is a 25% rise; reversing, \$50 → \$40 is a 20% drop — different bases give different percents.)
  • Decimal-point slips in conversion — 7% = 0.07, not 0.7. Sanity-check against the fraction (7/100).

Visuals to offer: None needed — the 100-grid is describable in words, and the part/whole = percent/100 skeleton carries the work.

Check for understanding (transfer):

  • #3.3.c1 "Convert 3/8 to a decimal and a percent." (Forces the trio fluency: 0.375 = 37.5%.)
  • #3.3.c2 "A price rises 25%, then falls 25%. Back to the start? Explain using the base idea." (No — the second base is larger.)
  • #3.3.c3 "\$50 → \$65 is what percent change, and which value is the base?"

Practice problems:

Find the part:

  1. #3.3.1 What is 25% of 60?
  2. #3.3.2 What is 40% of 150?

Find the percent:

  1. #3.3.3 18 is what percent of 72?
  2. #3.3.4 9 is what percent of 25?
  3. #3.3.5 2 is what percent of 3? (The answer doesn't terminate — decide how to report it.)

Find the whole:

  1. #3.3.6 21 is 70% of what number?
  2. #3.3.7 15 is 25% of what number?

Percent change:

  1. #3.3.8 A \$50 shirt goes up to \$65. What is the percent increase?
  2. #3.3.9 A town's population falls from 200 to 170. What is the percent decrease?

Two-step (real-world — preview of Unit 6):

  1. #3.3.10 An \$80 item is 15% off. What is the sale price?
  2. #3.3.11 A \$40 restaurant bill plus a 20% tip — what is the total?

Answer key: (percents reported with their % sign; dollar answers with \$)

  1. 0.25 × 60 = 15.
  2. 0.40 × 150 = 60.
  3. 18/72 = 0.25 = 25%.
  4. 9/25 = 0.36 = 36%.
  5. 0.70 · w = 21 ⇒ w = 30.
  6. 0.25 · w = 15 ⇒ w = 60.
  7. (65-50)/50 = 15/50 = 30% increase.
  8. (200-170)/200 = 30/200 = 15% decrease.
  9. Discount 0.15 × 80 = 12, so 80 - 12 = \$68 (or 0.85 × 80). Two steps: find the part, then subtract.
  10. Tip 0.20 × 40 = 8, so 40 + 8 = \$48 (or 1.20 × 40). Two steps: find the part, then add.
  11. 2/3 = 0.6666… = 66.66…%. Report it as 66.7% (rounded to a tenth) or exactly 66⅔% — both are acceptable; just don't write a clipped "66%" without saying you rounded. (A repeating decimal is a real answer; the choice is how precisely to report it.)

Wrap-up & interleaving

Consolidate: unit rate = "per one" = a constant rate of change = the seed of slope (Unit 5) — make sure this one sentence has landed; it is the reason this unit exists. Also lock in the why of cross-multiplication (clear both denominators) and the fraction ↔ decimal ↔ percent trio.

Mix back in: every proportion ends in a Unit 2 solve — keep cashing that in. Slip in a proportion that lands on a non-integer or a fraction (callback to Unit 2's fraction work and misconceptions.md §4). When a student finishes, throw one inverse problem back among direct ones so "is this even direct?" stays a live question, not a one-time lesson — and tie it to its constant: the direct case holds a constant ratio (y = kx, the seed of slope), the inverse case a constant product (xy = k).

Foreshadow: in Unit 4 (functions), a proportional relationship y = kx is the first clean function, and its constant k is the unit rate; in Unit 5 that same k is the slope of a line through the origin. Tell the student the bridge is built — they'll walk across it next.

Progress Card should note:

  • Solid on unit rate / unit price? Can they state it as a rate of change?
  • Do they explain cross-multiplication, or only execute it?
  • Did they correctly handle the inverse problem (3.2 #7), or default to direct?
  • Fraction ↔ decimal ↔ percent fluency: fluent, or still computing each conversion from scratch?
  • Percent change with the correct base?
