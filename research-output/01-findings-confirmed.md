## unit-01 — Foundations & the Language of Algebra  (quality: strong)

_Unit 1 is pedagogically excellent: the relational equals-sign front-loading, true/false/open sorting, four-tier order of operations, and "value not spelling" framing all align tightly with the strongest research and college-text practice, and every one of the 57 keyed answers plus all worked-example and prose numerics verify cleanly under sympy (re-checked twice — no math errors). The gaps are at the rigor/coverage edges of a college-level treatment: irrational numbers and the term "real numbers" are absent (so the number line is described as containing points the taxonomy can't name), absolute value is never introduced, grouping symbols are reduced to "parentheses" only, and the no-division-by-zero idea sitting inside the rational-number definition is never surfaced. None of these require precalculus scope-creep; they are standard Algebra-1 content present in OpenStax Elementary Algebra. Fixing them would lift an already-strong unit to full college rigor._

This creates an internal inconsistency with the unit's own central picture: line 103 asserts 'between any two integers there are infinitely many numbers' and 'algebra uses the whole line,' yet the taxonomy can only name the rational subset of that line. A learner is told the line is full but given no name for the points that are not rational, and the umbrella term 'real numbers' (the set algebra actually operates over) never appears.

Keep it light (no radical arithmetic — that is later), but close the loop so 'the whole line' has a name. This is core Algebra 1, not scope-creep.

## unit-02 — Solving Linear Equations  (quality: strong)

_Unit 2 (Solving Linear Equations) is pedagogically excellent: the balance metaphor, the "goes to zero / goes to one" anti-"cancel" language, the relentless substitution-check ritual, the getting-dressed ordering metaphor, and the heavy emphasis on the distributing-a-negative trap are all aligned with documented best practice and reflect real production care. Every one of the 95 computational items (answer keys, worked examples, spot-checks) was independently re-verified with sympy across two passes and is correct — there are no math errors. The gaps relative to a college-level text are not in correctness but in completeness and rigor of definitions: the unit never covers equations with no solution or infinitely many solutions (a standard Algebra 1 endpoint), never consolidates an explicit numbered general solving strategy, and gives an informal "term" definition that conflicts with the very sign-discipline the unit champions. A handful of smaller consistency/notation issues (out-of-order problem numbering in 2.5, an undefined "linear equation," uneven distributive-property statement) round out the list._

- **[major/coverage-gap] Unit 2 overall / coverage (compare Lesson 2.4 endpoint)** — The unit teaches only conditional equations (exactly one solution). It never presents equations with NO solution (contradictions, e.g. 2x+3=2x+5 → 3=5) or INFINITELY MANY solutions (identities, e.g. 2(x-1)=2x-2 → 0=0). This is a standard, explicitly taught endpoint of Algebra 1 in authoritative curricula and it follows naturally from Lesson 2.4 (variables on both sides): when you gather variable terms and they vanish, the student is currently given no framework for what a leftover 'true' or 'false' numeric statement means. An adult self-learner who later meets 3x+1=3x+1 or 5x=5x-1 will have no tool to interpret it.
    -> Add a short subsection at the end of Lesson 2.4 (or a Lesson 2.6) introducing the three outcomes. Use the variable-elimination signature the student already produces: if the variables drop out leaving a TRUE statement (e.g. 0=0), every number works → infinitely many solutions (identity); if they leave a FALSE statement (e.g. 6=1), no number works → no solution (contradiction); otherwise the one value is the solution (conditional). Worked pair to add (both sympy-verified): identity 2(x-1)=2x-2 → 2x-2=2x-2 → 0=0; contradiction 2x+3=2x+5 → 3=5. Keep scope to one-variable linear equations only — do NOT drift into systems.

The procedure is taught implicitly across five lessons and summarized only as a one-sentence habit ('do the same inverse move to both sides until x is alone, then check'). College texts make the algorithm explicit and reusable — this is a hallmark of the rigor/production quality the review targets, and it is exactly what lets a self-learner attack an unfamiliar equation (e.g. 3(x-2)+4 = 2(x+1) - x) with confidence.

Frame it as naming the pattern the student has already been using, not as new content, and cross-reference from 2.4/2.5. Do not characterize this as a college-level rigor deficiency.
