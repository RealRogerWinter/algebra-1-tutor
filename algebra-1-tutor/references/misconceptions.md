# Misconception Diagnostic Bank

A small set of misunderstandings causes a large share of algebra struggle. They're documented, persistent, and — crucially — invisible unless you look at the *specific* wrong answer. This bank helps you do three things:

1. **Read the tell** — recognize which misconception a wrong answer reveals.
2. **Confirm with a hinge question** — a quick question whose wrong answers each point at one misconception.
3. **Repair Socratically** — a cue that nudges the student to fix it themselves, ideally with a *new* representation rather than a louder repeat.

Golden rule: **diagnose before you re-teach**, and **never explain the same way twice** — switch to a metaphor, a number line, or a picture instead.

---

## 1. The equals sign: "do something" vs. "the same as"

**The misconception.** Many people read `=` as "the answer comes next" (an instruction to compute), not as "the left side has the same value as the right side" (a balance). This is the single most predictive early-algebra gap, because *every* equation-solving move depends on the relational meaning.

**Tells:**
- Fills `8 + 4 = ___ + 5` with **9** (computed 8+4, ignored the +5) or **17** (added everything).
- Uneasy with `7 = 3 + 4` or `5 = 5` — "that's backwards / there's nothing to do."
- Describes solving as "moving the number over and flipping the sign" with no sense of *why*.

**Hinge question:** "Fill the blank so this stays true: 8 + 4 = □ + 5." *(Correct: 7. Wrong answers 9 or 17 reveal the operational view.)*

**Socratic repair:** "Picture a balance scale. The left pan holds 8+4. For the scale to stay level, what must the right pan, □ + 5, weigh? ... So what's □?" Reinforce: whatever you do to one side, do to the other, *to keep it balanced*. Avoid the word "cancel" — say a term "goes to zero" (for +/−) or "goes to one" (for ×/÷), which is what actually happens.

## 2. Variables: the letter-as-object error

**The misconception.** Treating a letter as a label for a *thing* ("a = apples") rather than a *number we don't know yet*. Or assuming different letters must be different numbers, or that a letter is one fixed value rather than something that can vary.

**Tells:**
- Reads 5a as "5 apples" instead of "5 times some number."
- Says 5y with y=6 is "56" or just "5y."
- Thinks x and y can't ever be equal.
- Writes "6 students for every 1 professor" as 6s = p (the classic reversal).

**Hinge question:** "If y = 6, what is 5y?" *(Correct: 30. "56" or "5y" reveals letter-as-object.)* Follow with: "What is n + n + n? And what is n × n × n?" *(Expect 3n and n³; mixing them up is the same root error.)*

**Socratic repair:** "A variable is a closed box holding a number. Every x in a problem holds the *same* hidden number. If the box holds 6, what does 5x — five of that box — come out to? Let's test it." Use the mystery-box / labeled-cup metaphor (see `metaphors.md`).

## 3. Negative numbers and the negative sign

**The misconception.** Several distinct ones travel together:
- Applying the multiplication rule ("two negatives make a positive") to *addition*: -3 + -5 = +8.
- Mishandling subtracting a negative: 6 - (-2) = 4.
- Ordering negatives by size: thinking -5 > -2 because 5 > 2.
- Losing a sign when distributing: -(x - 4) = -x - 4.
- Confusing -3² with (-3)².

**Tells:** any of the above results.

**Hinge questions:**
- "-3 + (-5) = ?" *(Correct −8; +8 = additive/multiplicative confusion.)*
- "Which is greater, -5 or -2?" *(Correct −2; "−5" = magnitude-as-value.)*
- "6 - (-2) = ?" *(Correct 8; "4" = subtracting-a-negative not seen as adding.)*
- "-3² versus (-3)²?" *(Correct −9 and 9.)*

**Socratic repair:**
- *Addition/subtraction:* go to the **number line** (add positive → move right, add negative → move left) or **money & debt** ("-5 is owing $5; which is better off, owing $5 or owing $2?").
- *Subtracting a negative:* "Subtracting is one about-face; the number being subtracted is itself negative — a second about-face. Two flips point you... which way?" Or: "Taking away a debt makes you richer."
- *Distributing a negative:* "The minus is a -1 shaking hands with *everyone* inside the parentheses. What does -1 do to the -4?"
- Separate the **operation sign** from the **number's sign** out loud, and treat each negative as its own deliberate step. A substitution check (try x=1) catches most sign flips.

## 4. Fractions: bigger-number-means-bigger, and "reduce makes it smaller"

**The misconception.** Whole-number intuitions misfire on fractions: that a bigger denominator means a bigger value, that all fractions are less than 1, that "reducing" makes the *value* smaller, or that you operate on tops and bottoms independently. Fraction-magnitude understanding is the #1 long-run predictor of algebra success, so it's worth real care.

**Tells:**
- Says 1/6 > 1/4 ("6 > 4").
- Places 3/2 between 0 and 1; thinks 5/4 < 1.
- "Reducing 6/8 to 3/4 makes it smaller."
- Adds 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5 (added across).

**Hinge questions:**
- "Which is bigger, 1/4 or 1/6?" *(Correct 1/4.)*
- "Is 5/4 more or less than 1?" *(More.)*

**Socratic repair:** "Same pizza, split among 4 people or among 6 — whose slice is bigger?" For "all fractions < 1": "How many quarters make a whole? So where does the *fifth* quarter land?" For reducing: "6/8 and 3/4 name the *same point* on the number line — we just renamed it with smaller numbers; the value didn't move." Tie fractions to magnitude (number-line placement) every time, not just to procedures.

## 5. Order of operations: same-tier, left-to-right

**The misconception.** Reading PEMDAS as six strict ranks instead of four, where **multiply/divide share one rank** and **add/subtract share another**, each worked left to right. People over-apply "M before D" and "A before S."

**Tells:**
- 8 - 3 + 2 = 3 (did 3+2 first).
- 12 ÷ 2 × 3 = 2 (multiplied before dividing).
- 2 × 3² = 36 (multiplied before squaring).

**Hinge questions:**
- "8 - 3 + 2 = ?" *(Correct 7; "3" = addition-before-subtraction.)*
- "12 ÷ 2 × 3 = ?" *(Correct 18; "2" = multiplication-before-division.)*

**Socratic repair:** "Multiply and divide are *equal-rank partners* — like two words tied in importance, you read them left to right. Same for add and subtract. In 8 - 3 + 2, which comes first reading left to right?" Emphasize that PEMDAS is really **P, E, [MD], [AS]** — four tiers, not six.

## 6. Inequalities: forgetting the sign-flip (and treating them like equations)

**The misconception.** Solving an inequality exactly like an equation and forgetting that **multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative reverses the inequality**. Also a fuzzy sense of what a solution *set* means.

**Tells:**
- -2x < 6 ⇒ x < -3 (divided by −2, didn't flip).
- 5 - x > 2 ⇒ x > 3 (mishandled the -x).

**Hinge question:** "Solve -2x < 6." *(Correct x > -3; "x < -3" reveals the missing flip.)*

**Socratic repair (the "why," not the rule):** "Start with something obviously true: 2 < 3. Now multiply both sides by -1: you get -2 and -3. Is -2 < -3 still true? ... So what had to happen to the sign to keep it true?" Then verify the solved answer by **testing a number**: "Put x = 0 in the original — true? Is 0 inside your answer? If those disagree, something flipped." Testing a point is the self-check that catches it every time.

## 7. Algebraic structure / over-applied "tricks"

**The misconception.** Beginners chase memorized tricks instead of reading what an expression *says*: missing that p + 0.05p = 1.05p, or that (x+2)(x+3) has a middle term, or applying keyword shortcuts ("'of' means multiply") that later mislead.

**Tells:**
- (x+2)(x+3) = x² + 6 (forgot the middle terms — multiplied firsts and lasts only).
- Combines unlike terms: 3x + 2 = 5x.
- Word-problem keyword misreads.

**Hinge question:** "Expand (x+2)(x+3)." *(Correct x² + 5x + 6; "x²+6" = missing the cross terms.)*

**Socratic repair:** Use the **area model** — a rectangle (x+2) by (x+3) split into four cells. The four cells *are* the four products; the two middle cells are the 3x and 2x people forget. "Every part of the width meets every part of the height — how many little rectangles is that?" For unlike terms: "3x is three boxes; 2 is two loose units. Can you add boxes to loose units and get one kind of thing?"

---

## How to use this in the flow

- When an answer is wrong, **match the tell** here first, then pick the hinge question if you're unsure which misconception is live.
- Lead the repair with a **question and a fresh representation**, not a restatement.
- After a repair, confirm with a **transfer** problem (a new one of the same type), not the identical problem.
- Many of these are caught for free by the habit you teach anyway: **substitute the answer back and check.** Keep modeling it.
