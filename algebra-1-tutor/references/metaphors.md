# Metaphor & Reframe Library

When a first explanation doesn't land, the fix is a *different* representation — not the same words again. This file holds at least two distinct explanations for each hard concept, so you always have a second (and often a third) angle ready to offer. Use the phrasing "Would it help if I showed this a completely different way?" and then genuinely switch the picture.

A note on metaphors: every one of them eventually leaks. Use them to build intuition, then deliberately walk the student from the metaphor to the actual symbols (concrete → symbolic), and mention where the metaphor stops being exact if it would otherwise mislead.

---

## Equations & "do the same to both sides"

{#met.balance-scale} **A. The balance scale.** An equation is a scale that's level: the left pan and right pan weigh the same. To find a hidden weight, you can add or remove the same amount from *both* pans and it stays level. Solving = stripping away everything around x one balanced move at a time until x sits alone on its pan. *Where it leaks:* multiplying both sides by a negative has no clean "weight" picture — switch to the next metaphor for that.

{#met.getting-dressed} **B. Undoing a getting-dressed sequence (inverse operations).** To build 2x+3 from x you *multiply by 2, then add 3* — like putting on socks then shoes. To undo it you reverse the order and the action: *subtract 3, then divide by 2* — shoes off, then socks. This is why we peel off the +3 before the ×2. *Great for:* explaining the order of steps in two-step equations.

## Negative numbers

{#met.money-debt} **A. Money and debt.** Positive = cash you have; negative = debt you owe. -5 means owing $5. Adding a negative piles on debt; subtracting a negative is having a debt *forgiven*, so you end up richer — that's why 6 - (-2) = 8. "Who's better off, someone owing $5 or owing $2?" instantly fixes the -5 > -2 error.

{#met.number-line-walk} **B. Facing and walking on the number line.** Stand on a number line. A positive number = face right; the minus sign = "about-face" (turn around). Adding means walk forward in the way you're facing; subtracting means walk backward. Subtracting a negative is *two* about-faces, so you end up moving right (adding). *Great for:* making subtracting-a-negative feel inevitable rather than a rule to memorize.

## Variables

{#met.mystery-box} **A. The mystery box / labeled cup.** x is a closed box with a number hidden inside. Every x in the same problem holds the *same* number. 3x is three identical boxes. Solving is figuring out what's in the box. *Fixes:* letter-as-object errors and "different letters must differ."

{#met.reserved-seat} **B. The reserved seat.** A variable is a chair reserved for one specific (unknown) person — a placeholder. 5x is five chairs all reserved for that same person. When you "evaluate at x=6," the person finally sits down and you can count. *Great for:* the leap from "letter as label" to "letter as a number you'll later know."

## Functions

{#met.vending-machine} **A. The vending machine.** A function is a machine: put in a code (input), get exactly one item (output). The defining rule — *one output per input* — is just "press B4 and you always get the same snack." Two different buttons can dispense the same snack (two inputs, one output is fine), but one button can't dispense two different things (that would break the machine — and fail the vertical line test). *Fixes:* the "exactly one output" idea and the vertical line test.

{#met.recipe} **B. The recipe.** A function is a recipe: the same ingredients, run through the same steps, always yield the same dish. f(x) = 3x - 1 is the recipe "triple it, then subtract one." f(2) means "run 2 through the recipe." *Great for:* demystifying f(x) notation — it's just naming the recipe and saying what you fed it.

## Slope

{#met.stairs-ramp} **A. Stairs / a wheelchair ramp.** Slope is steepness: how much you rise for each step you take forward — rise over run. Gentle ramp = small slope; steep staircase = big slope. Downhill = negative. Flat landing = zero slope. A wall (straight up) = undefined (you can't move forward at all). *Fixes:* the four slope cases at once.

{#met.speed-rate} **B. Speed as a rate.** On a distance-vs-time graph, the slope *is* the speed — miles per hour, the change in distance per change in time. A steeper line means going faster. This ties slope directly back to the **unit rate** from Unit 3: slope is just a rate of change. *Great for:* connecting proportional reasoning to linear functions.

## The distributive property

{#met.flyers} **A. Handing out flyers.** The number outside the parentheses must hand a flyer to *everyone* inside — nobody gets skipped. 3(x+4): the 3 greets the x *and* the 4, giving 3x + 12. The classic error (3x+4) is forgetting to greet the second person. *Fixes:* partial distribution.

{#met.garden-area} **B. Area of a garden plot.** 3(x+4) is the area of a rectangle 3 tall and (x+4) wide. Split the width into an x part and a 4 part: you get a 3 × x plot plus a 3 × 4 plot — 3x + 12. This is the **area model**, and it scales straight up to multiplying binomials. *Great for:* a picture that survives all the way to FOIL and factoring.

## Multiplying binomials / FOIL

{#met.area-box} **A. The area box (2×2).** (x+2)(x+3) is a rectangle with sides x+2 and x+3. Cut it into four cells: x², 3x, 2x, 6. Add them: x² + 5x + 6. The two middle cells are exactly the terms people forget when they just do "firsts and lasts." *Strongly preferred over the FOIL acronym* because it generalizes and it shows *why* the middle term appears.

{#met.two-round-flyers} **B. Two rounds of handing out flyers.** (x+2)(x+3): first the x greets everyone in the second parenthesis (x·x and x·3), then the 2 greets everyone (2·x and 2·3). Distribution, done twice. *Great for:* students who already own the distributive "flyers" picture.

---

## Factoring

{#met.reverse-distribute} **A. Reverse-distributing / un-baking.** Factoring is multiplying run backward: you're handed the finished area (x² + 5x + 6) and must rebuild the rectangle's sides ((x+2)(x+3)). Same area box as multiplying — you just fill in the inside and deduce the edges. "What two numbers multiply to 6 and add to 5?" is the edge-finding question. *Great for:* making factoring feel like a familiar picture, not a new trick.

{#met.gcf-bags} **B. Pulling the common item out of every bag (GCF).** If every term carries the same factor, factoring it out is like noticing every gift bag contains a chocolate and setting all the chocolates aside in one pile out front: 6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3). *Great for:* GCF specifically.

## Quadratics having two solutions

{#met.squaring-loses-sign} **A. Squaring loses the sign.** 3² = 9 and (-3)² = 9 — squaring throws away whether you started positive or negative. So undoing a square (x² = 9) has to consider *both* origins: x = 3 or x = -3. The ± is recovering the information squaring destroyed. *Fixes:* "why are there two answers?"

{#met.parabola-two-roots} **B. The parabola crossing the axis twice.** A quadratic graphs as a U-shaped curve. The solutions are where it crosses the x-axis — and a U usually crosses *twice* (once going down, once coming back up). Seeing the picture makes "two solutions" obvious, and also explains the rare cases: one solution (the U just kisses the axis at its vertex) or none (the U floats entirely above or below). *Great for:* tying the algebra to a picture (pairs with the parabola artifact in `visuals.md`).

## How to deploy

- Offer the switch explicitly: "Want me to try a totally different picture for this?"
- Pick the metaphor that targets the *specific* confusion (the tables above note what each one fixes).
- Then **walk from the metaphor back to the symbols** — the goal is for the student to do the real algebra, with the picture as scaffolding they can later drop.
